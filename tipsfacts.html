This week's higlight piece: A maroon frock

This past week, I finished a maroon crochet frock for a six-month-old baby named Mira. Her mom wanted something special for an upcoming family gathering, something that felt festive, soft, and picture-worthy. We chose a deep maroon shade that looked absolutely lovely against Mira’s gentle complexion and tiny frame. Crocheting for babies always feels extra meaningful. Every stitch is done with care, knowing it’ll wrap around a little human who’s just starting out in the world. For Mira’s frock, I used a soft baby cotton yarn that’s gentle on the skin and breathable for warm weather. The design was kept simple through the body, with a slightly flared skirt and a scalloped trim at the bottom and sleeves to give it a finished, classic look.

The frock crocheted for Mira

The most delicate detail was a tiny handmade flower near the neckline just enough to make it feel special without overwhelming her small frame. Mira can’t speak yet, of course, but her wide-eyed look and calm smile while wearing the frock said everything. Her mom sent a photo of her lying on a blanket in the dress, fists balled up, eyes curious. It made my day. One thing I learned while making this piece is how crucial gentle shaping is when crocheting for babies. They’re always lying down or being held, so comfort matters just as much as appearance. The neckline, sleeves, and waist needed to stretch just enough without losing shape. This maroon frock turned out to be more than just an outfit, it became part of a memory. And that’s what I love about crochet: it’s not just about the object, but the moments we weave into them.

Fun Crochet Facts

Crochet Was Once a Status Symbol ▼
In 19th-century Europe, upper-class women often crocheted using fine thread and tiny hooks to show off their wealth and skill. It was seen as a refined, elegant pastime.
"Crochet" Means "Hook" in French ▼
The word crochet comes from the French word meaning “small hook,” which is exactly what you use to create stitches!
Queen Victoria Crocheted Too! ▼
Queen Victoria herself learned to crochet and even bought handmade crochet lace from Irish women during the famine to support them.
Crochet Uses Just One Hook▼
Unlike knitting, which uses two needles, crochet only needs one hook, making it more portable and great for on-the-go crafting.
There's a World Record for the Longest Crochet Chain▼
The longest crochet chain ever made was over 130 kilometers (more than 80 miles!) and was created by a woman from South Africa named Anne Vanier-Drüssel. .

Helpful Tips

Start with the Right Hook Size ▼
Choose a hook size that matches your yarn. Using a smaller hook will make tighter stitches, while a larger one gives a looser, more flowy fabric. Always check the label on your yarn for a recommended size.
Count Your Stitches ▼
Get into the habit of counting your stitches at the end of every row or round. It prevents mistakes from piling up and saves time fixing things later.
Don’t Skip the Swatch ▼
Crochet a small test swatch before starting a big project. This helps you check your gauge and adjust your hook size to match the pattern’s tension.
Weave in Ends as You Go ▼
Don’t leave all the yarn tails for the end. Weaving them in as you go makes the finishing process much easier and neater.
Block Your Finished Pieces ▼
Blocking (gently shaping and setting your crochet piece with steam or water) can make a huge difference in the final look, especially for garments and lace patterns.
